New Initiative · In Development

Community solar.
Power that pays the people.

LCLI is developing a community-owned solar farm in Laisamis, built on the same NLC-anchored governance as everything we do. The Chalbi and Kaisut zones hold some of the highest solar irradiance in East Africa. The model is simple: affordable electricity sold directly to local homes and businesses first, with surplus power sold to the national grid, and revenue flowing back through the Community Land Trust.

Revenue Stream 01

Direct to the community

A solar mini-grid supplying households, schools, clinics, boreholes, and small businesses at tariffs below standard retail rates. For many families in Laisamis this is first-time electricity: light after dark, cold-chain for medicine, power for enterprise. Community members are customers and owners, never just beneficiaries.

Revenue Stream 02

To the national grid

Surplus generation sold to Kenya Power under a Power Purchase Agreement, with EPRA generation licensing. Kenya has reopened PPAs under a competitive auction framework, and grid sales convert Laisamis sunshine into long-term community income that funds land registration, monitoring, and local services.

Phase 1

Feasibility & consent

Site selection on undisputed community land, documented FPIC through the CLMCs, solar resource and demand assessment, and EPRA/NEMA groundwork.

Phase 2

Community mini-grid

Build and operate the first mini-grid serving Laisamis homes and institutions, with youth trained as operators and technicians under our existing capacity programme.

Phase 3

Grid-tied expansion

Scale generation capacity and secure a PPA to sell surplus power to the national grid, anchoring long-term revenue for the Community Land Trust.

The Land

20,290 km² across
four ecological zones

From highland forest at 2,066m to the Chalbi Desert: one constituency, four distinct climate environments, one structured partnership access point.

Zone II: Highland Forest

Mt. Kulal & Marsabit highlands, up to 1,000mm rainfall, altitude to 2,066m. UNESCO Biosphere Reserve. Highest biodiversity, forest carbon potential.

Zone IV: Semi-Arid Mid-Slopes

Transitional zone. Soil carbon measurement, agri-climate data, solar irrigation potential. Gateway between highland and dryland systems.

Zone V/VI: Arid Bushed Grassland

300–900m. Primary deployment zone for solar, water harvesting, and remote sensing. Rangeland restoration carbon potential.

Chalbi / Kaisut Desert

Extreme aridity, highest solar irradiance in East Africa. Unique conditions for atmospheric water and off-grid energy technology deployment.

"ASAL regions cover 80% of Kenya and 40% of sub-Saharan Africa. Climate technology proven in Laisamis is technology proven for one of the world's largest underserved markets."

Governance

Community-owned.
Constitutionally anchored.

The governance failures that damaged high-profile African land projects follow one pattern: weak consent, no legal anchor, benefits captured by intermediaries. We built the structure to prevent exactly that.

1

National Land Commission (NLC)

Constitutional body under Article 67. Nine formal forums completed across all 6 wards with documented resolutions. Cannot be overridden by any individual, NGO, or investor.

2

Community Land Trust

Being established with elected ward trustees from all five communities. Benefit-sharing agreements embedded from the start. Community-owned, not NGO-managed.

3

Marsabit County Government

Formal institutional cover through County Land CECM. Ensures continuity beyond individual election cycles or political changes.

4

Politicians as conveners only

The Laisamis MP and Governor open community assemblies. Governance authority rests entirely with the NLC and the Community Land Trust.

⚠ Legal Clarity

~173,700 hectares are subject to an active High Court case and are excluded from any partnership commitment until resolved. All agreements use only undisputed, NLC-registered land.

About

Rooted in Laisamis.
Built for the long term.

Hamed Nalle is a community development practitioner and social innovator born and raised in Laisamis Constituency. He is the son of the late Ntepesi Nalle, a respected community leader whose legacy of service directly informs the values on which this initiative is built.

Through the Feiyah Action Network (FAN) and the Marsabit Youth Reform Program, Hamed has built deep grassroots networks across the constituency, working on youth empowerment, gender equality, and environmental conservation for over a decade.

LCLI sits alongside FAN as the climate and carbon dimension of a longer-running community development effort in northern Kenya. The two organizations share values, geography, and community trust built over years on the ground in Laisamis Ward and across the constituency.
